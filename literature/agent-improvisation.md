# Agent-Based Improvisation

## Overview
This subdomain explores how multi-agent systems, swarm intelligence, and agent-based models produce emergent creative behavior — connecting ALife's study of collective dynamics to improvisational processes.

## Swarm Intelligence & Collective Behavior

1. **Bonabeau, E., Dorigo, M., & Theraulaz, G. (1999). *Swarm Intelligence: From Natural to Artificial Systems*.** Oxford University Press.
   DOI: https://doi.org/10.1093/oso/9780195131581.001.0001
   - Foundational text on swarm intelligence. Demonstrates how simple local interaction rules produce complex collective behavior — a model for how improvisation emerges from individual actions without central control.

2. **Reynolds, C.W. (1987). Flocks, herds and schools: A distributed behavioral model.** *Computer Graphics (SIGGRAPH '87)*, 21(4), 25–34.
   DOI: https://doi.org/10.1145/37402.37406
   - The "boids" model showing how flocking emerges from three simple rules. Foundational for understanding emergent group behavior — analogous to ensemble improvisation where complex group dynamics arise from individual responsiveness.

3. **Camazine, S., Deneubourg, J.-L., Franks, N.R., et al. (2001). *Self-Organization in Biological Systems*.** Princeton University Press.
   DOI: https://doi.org/10.1515/9780691212920
   - Comprehensive account of self-organization in nature. Provides formal models for understanding how pattern and novelty emerge without central planning — the core mechanism of improvisation.

## Multi-Agent Creativity

4. **Saunders, R., & Gero, J.S. (2001). Artificial creativity: A synthetic approach to the study of creative behaviour.** In *Proc. Computational and Cognitive Models of Creative Design V*, 113–139.
   URL: https://mason.gmu.edu/~jgero/publications/2001/01SaundersGeroHeron.pdf
   - Proposes societies of curious agents that evaluate each other's creative outputs. Creativity emerges from social interaction between agents — directly paralleling ensemble improvisation.

5. **Saunders, R. (2012). Towards autonomous creative systems: A computational approach.** *Cognitive Computation*, 4(3), 216–225.
   DOI: https://doi.org/10.1007/s12559-012-9131-x
   - Develops computational models of creative societies. Agents develop individual creative styles through social interaction.

6. **Baker, B., Kanitscheider, I., Marber, T., et al. (2019). Emergent tool use from multi-agent autocurricula.** arXiv:1909.07528.
   URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/1909.07528
   - OpenAI's hide-and-seek agents develop increasingly sophisticated strategies through competition. Demonstrates how multi-agent interaction produces unbounded novelty — a computational analogue to improvisational escalation.

7. **Sukhbaatar, S., Szlam, A., & Fergus, R. (2016). Learning multiagent communication with backpropagation.** In *NeurIPS 2016*.
   URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/1605.07736
   - Agents learn to communicate through interaction. Emergent communication parallels how improvisers develop shared musical/gestural language.

## Agent Ethology & Behavioral Emergence

8. **Hu, B.A., et al. (2024). Agent ethology: Toward ethological study of AI agent behavior.** (Working paper, Reality Design Lab.)
   URL: https://github.com/realitydeslab/Agent-Ethology
   - Proposes studying AI agents using ethological methods from animal behavior research. Observing agent "improvisation" in naturalistic multi-agent environments.

9. **Bedau, M.A. (2003). Artificial life: Organization, adaptation and complexity from the bottom up.** *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 7(11), 505–512.
   DOI: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tics.2003.09.012
   - Overview of ALife's bottom-up approach to studying emergence and self-organization. The same principles underlie both agent-based OE and collective improvisation.

10. **Ikegami, T., & Hashimoto, T. (2002). Emergence of net-grammar in communicating agents.** *BioSystems*, 65(2–3), 143–154.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1016/S0303-2647(02)00012-4
    - Studies how shared grammatical structures emerge from agent communication — analogous to how musical/improvisational conventions emerge in performing groups.

## Swarm Art & Creative Agents

11. **Bisig, D., & Unemi, T. (2010). Swarm-based interaction in artistic applications.** In *Proc. ALIFE XII*, 420–427. MIT Press.
    URL: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/228943270
    - Uses swarm algorithms for interactive artistic installations. Audience interaction with swarm dynamics creates improvised visual art.

12. **Blackwell, T., & Bentley, P. (2002). Improvised music with swarms.** In *Proc. Congress on Evolutionary Computation (CEC)*, 1462–1467. IEEE.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1109/CEC.2002.1004458
    - **KEY PAPER.** Directly applies swarm algorithms to musical improvisation. Multiple swarm agents generate music in real-time, producing emergent musical structures.

13. **Blackwell, T. (2007). Swarming and music.** In *Evolutionary Computer Music*, 194–217. Springer.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1007/978-1-84628-600-1_9
    - Extends swarm-music work. Explores how attractor dynamics in swarm systems produce musical tension and resolution analogous to improvised music.

14. **Eigenfeldt, A., & Pasquier, P. (2010). Realtime generation of navigable musical compositions.** In *Proc. NIME 2010*, 244–249.
    URL: https://www.nime.org/proceedings/2010/nime2010_244.pdf
    - Multi-agent system generating navigable musical compositions in real-time. Agents act as virtual musicians with individual personalities.

## Emergence & Safety in MAS

15. **Hammond, L., Fox, J., Sherstan, C., et al. (2024). Emergence in multi-agent systems: A safety perspective.** arXiv:2408.04514.
    URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.04514
    - Analyzes emergent behavior in multi-agent systems from a safety perspective. Relevant to understanding when agent-based improvisation might produce unexpected/undesirable outcomes.

16. **Rahwan, I., Cebrian, M., Obradovich, N., et al. (2019). Machine behaviour.** *Nature*, 568(7753), 477–486.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-019-1138-y
    - Foundational paper proposing the scientific study of machine behavior using frameworks from behavioral science. Argues AI agents should be studied as behavioral entities — directly supports agent ethology as a lens for understanding agent improvisation.

17. **Buehler, M.J. (2025). MusicSwarm: Biologically inspired intelligence for music composition.** *Advanced Intelligent Systems*. arXiv:2509.11973.
    URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.11973
    - Decentralized swarm of frozen LLM agents coordinate via stigmergic signals to compose coherent music. Creativity emerges from system-level organization, not individual agents — a computational model of collective musical improvisation.

18. **Chen, B., et al. (2023). AgentVerse: Facilitating multi-agent collaboration and exploring emergent behaviors.** arXiv:2308.10848.
    URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.10848
    - Framework for multi-agent LLM collaboration. Documents emergent collaborative behaviors including role specialization and negotiation — analogous to ensemble improvisation dynamics.

19. **Gupta, A., et al. (2025). MAEBE: Multi-agent emergent behavior framework.** arXiv:2506.03053.
    URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.03053
    - Framework for studying emergent behavior in multi-agent systems. Provides tools for observing and characterizing emergent dynamics relevant to agent-based improvisation.

## Key Themes for Open-Endedness Connection

- **Emergence from local rules**: Both swarm behavior and ensemble improvisation produce complex emergent patterns from simple local interactions
- **No central control**: Neither improvisation nor swarm behavior has a conductor — coordination emerges
- **Autocurricula**: Multi-agent competition/cooperation drives unbounded novelty (Baker et al.) — analogous to improvisational escalation
- **Agent personality**: Creative agents develop individual styles through social interaction — like improvisers developing voices
- **Swarm music**: Direct application of ALife principles to musical improvisation (Blackwell)
