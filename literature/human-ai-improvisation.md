# Human-AI Improvisation

## Overview
This subdomain covers systems where humans and AI agents engage in real-time creative collaboration — particularly music, dance, and visual art. It bridges improvisation theory with AI system design.

## Pioneering Systems

1. **Lewis, G.E. (2000). Too many notes: Computers, complexity and culture in Voyager.** *Leonardo Music Journal*, 10, 33–39.
   DOI: https://doi.org/10.1162/096112100570585
   - Voyager (1987–) is among the first systems designed for genuine human-computer improvisation. Lewis's system analyzes human input and generates independent musical responses, treating the computer as a creative agent with its own "subjectivity" — not a tool or accompanist.

2. **Hoffman, G., & Weinberg, G. (2011). Interactive improvisation with a robotic marimba player.** *Autonomous Robots*, 31(2–3), 133–153.
   DOI: https://doi.org/10.1007/s10514-011-9237-0
   - Shimon, a four-armed robotic marimba player, improvises in real-time with human musicians. Uses anticipatory action based on musical analysis. Key contribution: embodied robotic musicianship where physical gesture contributes to musical communication.

3. **Weinberg, G., & Driscoll, S. (2006). Robot-human interaction with an anthropomorphic percussionist.** In *Proc. CHI 2006*, 1229–1232. ACM.
   DOI: https://doi.org/10.1145/1124772.1124955
   - Haile, an early robotic drummer designed for improvisational interaction. Explores how robots can imitate, transform, and improvise alongside human drummers.

4. **Weinberg, G., Raman, A., & Mallikarjuna, T. (2009). Interactive jamming with Shimon: A social robotic musician.** In *Proc. HRI 2009*, 233–234. ACM.
   DOI: https://doi.org/10.1145/1514095.1514152
   - Demonstrates social aspects of robotic musical improvisation, including visual gestures and beat synchronization.

## Dance & Movement

5. **Davis, N., Hsiao, C.-P., Yashraj Singh, K., Li, L., & Magerko, B. (2016). Empirically studying participatory sense-making in abstract drawing with a co-creative cognitive agent.** In *Proc. IUI 2016*. ACM.
   DOI: https://doi.org/10.1145/2856767.2856795
   - Studies how humans make sense of co-creative interaction, using drawing as medium. Informs design of LuminAI dance system.

6. **Weinberg, G., & Driscoll, S. (2020). Visualizing improvisation in LuminAI, an AI partner for co-creative dance.** In *Proc. MOCO 2020*. ACM.
   DOI: https://doi.org/10.1145/3401956.3404258
   - Explores visualization of how LuminAI learns gesture relationships and applies domain knowledge for dance improvisation.

7. **Cuan, K. (2019). OUTPUT: Performing AI dance.** In *Proc. MOCO 2019*. ACM.
   DOI: https://doi.org/10.1145/3347122.3371155
   - Artist-researcher documenting practice of performing dance generated from/with AI. Reflects on embodied experience of human-AI movement improvisation.

## Co-Creative Frameworks

8. **Rezwana, J., & Maher, M.L. (2023). Designing creative AI partners with COFI.** *ACM TOCHI*, 30(5), 1–28.
   DOI: https://doi.org/10.1145/3519026
   - COFI (Co-Creative Framework for Interaction) models how humans and AI collaborate, including initiative timing, contribution types, and communication modes. Applicable to improvisational systems.

9. **Deterding, S., Hook, J., Fiebrink, R., Gillies, M., Gow, J., et al. (2017). Mixed-initiative creative interfaces.** In *Proc. CHI EA 2017*, 628–635. ACM.
   DOI: https://doi.org/10.1145/3027063.3027072
   - Workshop report on mixed-initiative creative interfaces where both human and AI take creative initiative — the fundamental structure of improvisational collaboration.

10. **Muller, M., & Weisz, J.D. (2022). Human-AI co-creation.** In *Springer Handbook of Human-Computer Interaction*. Springer.
    URL: https://link.springer.com/rwe/10.1007/978-981-97-8440-0_76-1
    - Establishes human-AI co-creation as a new interaction paradigm that moves beyond input-output models to embrace turn-taking, improvisation, and social collaboration.

## Musical AI Improvisation

11. **Pachet, F. (2003). The Continuator: Musical interaction with style.** *Journal of New Music Research*, 32(3), 333–341.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1076/jnmr.32.3.333.16861
    - The Continuator learns a musician's style in real-time and generates continuations. Creates the illusion of a musical dialogue partner.

12. **Assayag, G., & Dubnov, S. (2004). Using factor oracles for machine improvisation.** *Soft Computing*, 8(9), 604–610.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1007/s00500-004-0385-4
    - OMax system uses factor oracles (string matching data structures) for real-time musical improvisation, capturing and recombining patterns from live performance.

13. **Bretan, M., Oore, S., Shlizerman, E., & Weinberg, G. (2017). Deep learning for robotic jazz improvisation.** In *Proc. International Florida AI Research Society Conference (FLAIRS)*. AAAI Press.
    URL: https://www.aaai.org/ocs/index.php/FLAIRS/FLAIRS17/paper/view/15530
    - Applies deep learning to Shimon's improvisation, enabling generation of novel musical phrases based on learned jazz patterns.

14. **Savery, R., Rose, R., & Weinberg, G. (2021). Creative AI and musicking robots.** *Frontiers in Robotics and AI*, 8, 631752.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.3389/frobt.2021.631752
    - Comprehensive review of AI-driven robots co-creating with human musicians. Examines technical solutions, artistic potential, and the EMRv1 case study.

15. **Huang, Y., et al. (2025). Revival: Collaborative artistic creation through human-AI interactions in musical creativity.** arXiv:2503.15498.
    URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.15498
    - Real-time co-creative improvisation between percussionist, electronic artist, and AI agents trained on deceased composers' works. Recent state-of-the-art in human-AI musical collaboration.

## Directly Relevant to This Project

16. **Hu, Botao 'Amber' (2025). On improvisation and open-endedness: Insights for experiential AI.** arXiv:2511.00529.
    URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.00529
    - **THIS IS OUR WIP PAPER being expanded into a full CHI 2027 submission.** Interviews 6 improvisation experts (dance, music, contact improv). Identifies themes: active listening, being in the time, embracing the unknown, non-judgmental flow, balancing structure and surprise, imaginative metaphor, empathy/trust/care, playfulness. Sole author: Botao 'Amber' Hu.

17. **Ladenheim, K., et al. (2024). Human-AI co-dancing: Evolving cultural heritage through collaborative choreography with generative virtual characters.** In *Proc. MOCO 2024*. ACM.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1145/3658852.3661317
    - Combines human dancers with AI virtual partners powered by algorithms from traditional Thai dance. Real-time movement generation creates improvisational partnership grounded in cultural heritage.

18. **Cetin, M., et al. (2025). AI See, You See: Human-AI musical collaboration in augmented reality.** In *Proc. CHI EA 2025*. ACM.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1145/3706599.3720052
    - Tests co-creative improvisation with two human musicians and an AI agent, investigating how AR interfaces affect perception and roles of AI in improvisation.

19. **Spence, J. (2025). Artificial intelligence in dance choreography: A practice-as-research exploration of human-AI co-creation using ChatGPT-4.** *International Journal of Performance Arts and Digital Media*, 21(2).
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1080/14794713.2025.2515754
    - Practice-as-research exploration of LLM-based dance co-creation. Examines how text-based AI interaction translates into embodied improvisational practice.

## Key Themes

- **Agency and initiative**: Who leads in human-AI improvisation? Mixed-initiative and turn-taking models
- **Embodiment matters**: Physical robots (Shimon, Haile) create different improvisational dynamics than screen-based systems
- **Style and personality**: Effective AI improvisers develop recognizable creative voices (Lewis's Voyager, Pachet's Continuator)
- **Real-time responsiveness**: Improvisation demands millisecond-level perception-action coupling
- **Social dynamics**: Improvisation is not just technical — it involves trust, risk, vulnerability (Hu, 2025)
