# Musical Open-Endedness

## Overview
This subdomain connects musical improvisation — particularly free jazz, live coding, and generative music — to concepts of open-endedness. Music provides some of the richest examples of processes that generate unbounded novelty in real-time.

## Free Jazz & Open Form

1. **Bailey, D. (1993). *Improvisation: Its Nature and Practice in Music* (2nd ed.).** Da Capo Press.
   URL: https://www.hachettebookgroup.com/titles/derek-bailey/improvisation/9780306805288/
   - Comprehensive survey of improvisation across musical traditions. Distinguishes "idiomatic" (within a style) from "non-idiomatic" (free) improvisation. Free improvisation represents the most open-ended musical practice.

2. **Lewis, G.E. (1996). Improvised music after 1950: Afrological and Eurological perspectives.** *Black Music Research Journal*, 16(1), 91–122.
   DOI: https://doi.org/10.2307/779379
   - Influential theorization of two traditions in free improvisation: Afrological (rooted in African-American musical traditions, social interaction) and Eurological (rooted in European experimentalism, indeterminacy). Essential for understanding the cultural contexts of musical open-endedness.

3. **Borgo, D. (2005). *Sync or Swarm: Improvising Music in a Complex Age*.** Continuum.
   URL: https://www.bloomsbury.com/us/sync-or-swarm-9780826418845/
   - Analyzes free improvisation through complexity theory. Identifies how improvisers navigate the "edge of chaos" — maintaining enough structure to communicate while remaining open to surprise. Directly models the OE tension between order and novelty.

4. **Corbett, J. (2016). *A Listener's Guide to Free Improvisation*.** University of Chicago Press.
   DOI: https://doi.org/10.7208/9780226353906
   - Guide to free improvisation as listening practice. Emphasizes that free improvisation is truly open-ended — no one knows where the music will go, including the performers.

## Live Coding

5. **McLean, A. (2014). Making programming languages to dance to: Live coding with TidalCycles.** In *Proc. 2nd ACM SIGPLAN International Workshop on Functional Art, Music, Modelling & Design (FARM)*, 63–70. ACM.
   DOI: https://doi.org/10.1145/2633638.2633647
   - Introduces TidalCycles as a live coding language designed for improvised music performance. Programming becomes a real-time improvisational act.

6. **McLean, A., & Dean, R.T. (Eds.). (2018). *The Oxford Handbook of Algorithmic Music*.** Oxford University Press.
   DOI: https://doi.org/10.1093/oxfordhb/9780190226992.001.0001
   - Comprehensive handbook covering algorithmic composition, live coding, and generative music. Multiple chapters address improvisation as algorithmic practice.

7. **Blackwell, A.F., McLean, A., Noble, J., & Rohrhuber, J. (2014). Collaboration and learning through live coding.** In *Proc. PPIG 2014*.
   URL: https://ppig.org/papers/2014-ppig-25th-blackwell/
   - Analyzes live coding as collaborative improvisational practice. Live coding communities (TOPLAP, Algorave) as sites of collective creative emergence.

8. **Collins, N., McLean, A., Rohrhuber, J., & Ward, A. (2003). Live coding in laptop performance.** *Organised Sound*, 8(3), 321–330.
   DOI: https://doi.org/10.1017/S135577180300030X
   - Foundational paper on live coding as performance practice. Argues live coding is inherently improvisational — the performer writes code in real-time, responding to what emerges.

## Generative Music & Evolutionary Approaches

9. **Biles, J.A. (1994). GenJam: A genetic algorithm for generating jazz solos.** In *Proc. ICMC 1994*. ICMA.
   URL: https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/GenJam%3A-A-Genetic-Algorithm-for-Generating-Jazz-Biles/9b0be5e02f35b3f10a22c3bcd66d18bc7a7ccb1c
   - Pioneering evolutionary approach to jazz improvisation. Uses genetic algorithm with interactive fitness evaluation to evolve jazz solos. Bridges evolutionary computation and musical improvisation.

10. **Miranda, E.R., & Biles, J.A. (Eds.). (2007). *Evolutionary Computer Music*.** Springer.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1007/978-1-84628-600-1
    - Collection covering evolutionary approaches to music generation. Multiple chapters address improvisation, including swarm-based approaches (Blackwell).

11. **McCormack, J. (2005). Open problems in evolutionary music and art.** In *Applications of Evolutionary Computing*, 428–436. Springer.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-540-32003-6_43
    - Identifies open problems in evolutionary art/music, including achieving genuine open-endedness rather than convergence.

12. **Eno, B. (1996). *A Year with Swollen Appendices*.** Faber & Faber.
    URL: https://www.faber.co.uk/product/9780571179954-a-year-with-swollen-appendices/
    - Contains Eno's influential concept of "generative music" — music that evolves continuously and never repeats. Generative music systems are designed for open-endedness.

## Algorithmic Improvisation

13. **Assayag, G., Bloch, G., Chemillier, M., Cont, A., & Dubnov, S. (2006). OMax brothers: A dynamic topology of agents for improvisation.** In *Proc. NIME 2006*.
    URL: https://www.nime.org/proceedings/2006/nime2006_225.pdf
    - OMax system for real-time machine improvisation using factor oracles. Multiple agents create an "improvisational topology."

14. **Rowe, R. (1993). *Interactive Music Systems: Machine Listening and Composing*.** MIT Press.
    URL: https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262681087/interactive-music-systems/
    - Early comprehensive treatment of interactive music systems that listen and respond — the technical infrastructure for human-AI musical improvisation.

15. **Pachet, F. (2012). Musical virtuosity and creativity.** In *Computers and Creativity*, 115–146. Springer.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-642-31727-9_5
    - Explores computational musical creativity through the lens of virtuosity and improvisation. The Continuator and Flow Machines projects.

16. **Buehler, M.J. (2025). MusicSwarm: Biologically inspired intelligence for music composition.** *Advanced Intelligent Systems*. arXiv:2509.11973.
    URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.11973
    - Decentralized swarm of LLM agents compose music via stigmergic coordination. Superior diversity and creativity metrics. A direct computational model of how musical open-endedness can emerge from collective improvisation without central control.

17. **Borgo, D. (2006). Sync or swarm: Musical improvisation and the complex dynamics of group creativity.** *Journal of Consciousness Studies*, 13(7–8), 52–64.
    URL: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/221350614
    - Journal article version of Borgo's book-length argument. Maps improvisation onto complexity theory: the "edge of chaos" between synchronization (lock-in) and swarming (chaos) is where open-ended musical creativity occurs.

## Key Themes for Open-Endedness Connection

- **Free improvisation as maximally open-ended music**: No score, no predetermined structure — the closest musical analogue to OE
- **Live coding as improvisational algorithm design**: Programmers write open-ended generative systems in real-time
- **Evolutionary music**: Genetic algorithms and interactive evolution directly connect evolutionary OE to musical creation
- **Edge of chaos**: Borgo's analysis shows improvisation navigating the same phase transition between order and novelty that characterizes OE systems
- **Generative music**: Eno's vision of ever-evolving, non-repeating music is essentially a design specification for open-endedness
