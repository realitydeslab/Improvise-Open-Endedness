## Expected Submission Format

Based on CHI 2025–2026 precedent:

- **Format:** ACM single-column for review; dual-column for camera-ready
- **Length:** No strict page limit, but length must be commensurate with contribution. CHI encourages shorter, focused papers. Typical full papers: 10–20 pages in camera-ready format.
- **Anonymization:** Double-blind review required
- **Template:** ACM TAPS-compatible LaTeX or Word template
- **Accessibility:** Must follow SIGCHI Guide to an Accessible Submission

## Review Criteria (from CHI 2026 Guide to Reviewing)

Primary criterion: **Does this submission provide a strong contribution to the field of HCI?**

Specific dimensions:
1. **Significance** — Why the contribution and benefit matter
2. **Originality** — What new ideas or approaches are introduced
3. **Research Quality** — How rigorous and clear are the research processes
4. **Presentation Clarity** — Is the paper well-written and structured
5. **Relevant Previous Work** — Is prior work adequately reviewed

## Contribution Types Recognized at CHI

CHI accepts diverse contribution types:
- **Empirical** — Studies of human behavior, user studies, field studies, qualitative research
- **Theoretical/Conceptual** — Frameworks, models, taxonomies, design spaces
- **Systems** — Novel interactive systems, tools, architectures
- **Methodological** — New research methods or approaches
- **Design** — Design explorations, research-through-design, design implications
- **Argument/Position** — Provocative essays with well-supported claims
- **Survey/Meta** — Systematic reviews, meta-analyses

## Tracks

- **Papers** (main track) — Full archival contributions
- **alt.chi** — Provocative, boundary-pushing work (separate CFP)
- **Late-Breaking Work** (posters/demos) — Shorter, in-progress work
- **Workshops** — Half/full-day collaborative sessions
- **Journals** — TOCHI and PACM HCI articles presented at CHI

## What Makes a Strong CHI Paper

Based on reviewing guidelines and best paper analysis:

1. **Clear, specific contribution statement** — Reviewers must immediately understand what's new
2. **Appropriate method for the claim** — Qualitative claims need qualitative rigor; system claims need evaluation
3. **Design implications** — CHI strongly values actionable implications for design/practice
4. **Engagement with HCI community** — Must speak to HCI audience, not just borrow from adjacent fields
5. **Reflexivity in qualitative work** — CHI 2026 scoping review found ~11% of papers use Reflexive TA, but quality varies widely; rigorous reflexive practice is increasingly expected
6. **Length proportional to contribution** — Overlong papers are rejected
